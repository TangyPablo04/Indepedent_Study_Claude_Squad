These charts illustrate what our findings could have looked like with a full sample of ~400 respondents. All values are simulated based on our theoretical framework and plausible effect sizes from comparable studies. They are not findings — they are a demonstration of the analytical framework we designed.

Average AI aptitude scores by major

Self-perception (1–5 Likert) and actual demonstrated score (0–4 composite) shown side-by-side. A consistent gap across all majors indicates widespread overestimation of AI readiness. MIS students score highest on both dimensions; Management students score lowest.

Figure 5

K-means cluster analysis — student AI profiles (k = 3)

Unsupervised clustering reveals three distinct student archetypes based on the combination of self-perception and actual AI score. Clusters were identified using k-means with k=3, selected via elbow method. Each cluster represents a meaningfully different relationship to AI readiness.

Innovators (n ≈ 28) Skeptics (n ≈ 52) Novices (n ≈ 65)
Three clusters visible. Innovators upper-right quadrant. Skeptics mid-left. Novices lower-left.

Innovators

High self-perception · High actual score
Predominantly MIS and upper-division Finance. Comfortable applying AI to complex business tasks. Likely to be early adopters in the workforce.

Skeptics

High self-perception · Moderate actual score
Mixed majors, often sophomores and juniors. Overestimate their readiness relative to demonstrated performance — the most common pattern.

Novices

Low self-perception · Low actual score
Largely freshmen, Management and Marketing majors. Aware of their limitations. Highest potential for growth with targeted AI literacy instruction.
